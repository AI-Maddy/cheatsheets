Overview

Advanced Audio Coding (AAC) is a lossy digital audio compression standard designed
to be the successor to MP3. Standardized as part of MPEG-2 (ISO/IEC 13818-7) in 1997
and later extended in MPEG-4 (ISO/IEC 14496-3), AAC achieves better sound quality than
MP3 at the same bit rate through more sophisticated psychoacoustic modeling and encoding
techniques.

AAC is widely used across streaming platforms, mobile devices, broadcasting (DAB+, DVB),
and digital media distribution. It supports a wide range of bit rates (8 kbps to 320+ kbps),
multiple sampling rates (8 kHz to 96 kHz), and up to 48 full-bandwidth audio channels.
The format's efficiency and quality have made it the de facto standard for digital audio
in the modern era, replacing MP3 in most new applications.

Key Features

- Better compression efficiency: ~30% improvement over MP3 at equivalent quality
- Wide bit rate support: 8 kbps (voice) to 320+ kbps (high fidelity)
- Multichannel support: Up to 48 full-bandwidth channels
- Multiple profiles: AAC-LC (Low Complexity), HE-AAC (High Efficiency), HE-AAC v2
- Enhanced psychoacoustic modeling: Temporal Noise Shaping (TNS), Perceptual Noise Substitution (PNS)
- Flexible sampling rates: 8 kHz to 96 kHz
- Built-in error resilience: Error concealment tools for transmission
- Spectral Band Replication (SBR): Extends bandwidth in HE-AAC variants
- Parametric Stereo (PS): Further compression in HE-AAC v2

1. AAC-LC (Low Complexity): Most widely used, good quality-to-complexity ratio
2. HE-AAC (AAC+ or aacPlus): Adds SBR for efficient low bit rate encoding (32-64 kbps)
3. HE-AAC v2: Adds Parametric Stereo for even lower bit rates (16-48 kbps)
4. AAC-LD (Low Delay): For real-time two-way communication applications
5. AAC-ELD (Enhanced Low Delay): Combines low delay with SBR for better quality
